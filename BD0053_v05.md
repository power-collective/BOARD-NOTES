# BD0053_v05

Found in: a wide array of "Geekbar Galaxy" / "Geekbar Pulse X" / -type things, with big ole LED arrays driven by a 19x6 LED driver chip.

Commonly used LED driver chips include:

- most common is the [PLS916H with no official documentation but a reverse-engineered driver for Arduino](https://gist.github.com/The5thZone/fcb294b999651d99634e3481fc5ee4ed)
- and rarely the [AiP33629 16x9 i2c LED driver with documentation available only in Chinese](http://www.i-core.cn/upload/file1738999785557/instructions/AiP33629%EF%BC%88%E4%B8%AD%E6%96%87%EF%BC%89.pdf)
- and at least on one single model a [ZW3568F // 4EAT449]() with no documentation or previous reports

## OUTPUTs

- PB3 - active low, ch2 on strong
- PA11 - active low, ch1 on strong
- PA9 = CLK (Serial Clock)
- PA10 = MOSI/DIN (Data Out)
- PA15 - active low, power control for LED array

## CHARGING

- PF0 - U4 ~CHG - "Open-Drain Charge Status Output. When the battery is charging, the CHRG pin is pulled low by an internal NMOS." - ext pull-up 1k
- PF1 - U4 Current - "Charge Current Program. The charge current is programmed by connecting a 1% resistor(RISET) to ground. " (pull high to disable charger?) - ext pull-down 2.4k
- PB5 - U1 ~CHG - see above, ext pull-up
- PB4 - U1 Current - see above, ext pull-down

## INPUTS

- **Digital Puff Sensor**: PA3 (active-high, pull-down enabled)
- **3-Way Switch**: PA2 - detectable via ADC (ADC_IN2) or <s>via GPIO (pull-up / pull-down) on input</s> NB: GPIO pull trick only works on BD0027
  - Position 0: Heater OFF (0.00V ADC reading)
  - Position 1: Heater HIGH (3.30V ADC reading - full VDD)
  - Position 2: Heater LOW/Standby (0.30V ADC reading)
- **ADC Channels**: CH2, CH8, CH9, CH12 (4 channels active)
  - PA2: Analog mode (likely ADC_IN2) - monitors switch position
  - PB0: Analog mode (likely ADC_IN8) - monitors output 1 voltage
  - PB1: Analog mode (likely ADC_IN9) - monitors output 2 voltage
  - (CH12 is the internal vref)

## REALLY ANNOYING QUIRK:

### VIN detect

No way(?) as-of-yet discovered to detect when the battery is fully charged and the PCB is attached to VBUS/VIN.

Have tried mucking around with combinations of LP4068 pins, in (input pull-up, input pull-down, input floating, output high, output low) and nothing seems to work reliably.

Is worth flagging that CHG seems to go low briefly (1-5 seconds) on attaching power, but this is not a reliable signal as it can be pretty easily missed.

### Toasting power switches when board's in BOD

Haven't exactly figured out this one, but have noticed a few times when handling the PCB with a low battery and a load attached to the V+1 / V+2 outputs, that the pFET will spontaneously catch fire.

Not an issue if the board is handled either with VBATT > VBOD (3v3-ish) or with no battery attached.
Pretty sure it's the GPIO all going high-Z in brownout, and then the slight conductance of human skin driving the pFET into the ohmic region, where it will quickly dissipate multiple watts of power in a SOT23-3 package.

## Favorite modifications:

### VIN detect

A simple modification is to give up one of the high-power outputs and jump ie) V+1 to either "V" (VIN testpoint) or FS1/FS2, which gives a simple PB0=high when VIN is connected. It's a little sus to connect 5V to a microcontroller powered via 4.2V, but there's a 1KO series resistor which limits current quite nicely.

### Status LED

The PCB has *no* visible outputs without the external LED array attached, which is a) hard to clean goo off of, bulky / awkward, and hard to drive directly.

The ZIF connector (0.5mm pitch?) used to attach the ribbon cable to the external LED array can be pretty trivially desoldered. Push a tip with a blob of solder from the inside to the outside of the board. Make sure the switch is in "OFF" or there is no battery connected to avoid shorting out Q17, the low-current pFET used to control power to the LED array. With this connector desoldered, there's a very easy to scratch trace between the V+ pins on the connector footprint, and an 0603 LED and 0603 series resistor can trivially be bodged between ie) the corner GND pins of the connector, and the center of that trace. If this is hard to follow, see the picture below. A 100-1k 0603 resistor works fine, and then settting PA15 low will turn on the newly added LED.

### N-FET bodge

When a desired device needs a low-side switch (in my case, a USB desk lamp I was rebuilding), a nFET can be bodged onto the PCB, with the GATE driven by the drain of the pre-existing pFET (V1+/V2+), source to ground (scrape solder mask off a nearby ground pour), and drain to the load. A pull-down resistor of 1k-10k is required to drain the charge stored on the nFET gate (pFET drain can't conduct to ground). A 2kO resistor (what I used) with a 200pF gate capacitance like found in the NTR4501N (what I had on hand) will turn off completely in 2microseconds.
